    Gates

    I can see both sides, though I also share concerns about PCs being able to use them, for a few reasons. The big one being, "how do we write around it?"

    I mean, we normally try to shy away from person-to-person combat except with the most violent of villains, but still, the PCs having access to such a Super Mode becomes a problem when you're trying to write a villain that could be a credible threat, even in melee combat. If the character(s) s/he is supposed to be opposed to can burst into the first Gate at the first sign of serious threat, then that either diminishes the villain and makes them much weaker in comparison or requires the villain to suddenly develop newer, possibly nastier, methods and/or powers. This can lead to escalation as people try to come up with new ways for villains to stand out, and actually feel like villains, and not overhyped minions. It also makes balancing non-Aurics, Aurics, and Gate Aurics much harder in terms of what villains are threatening to them.

    Gates

    Well, that's part of it. I'm also concerned that our statement of "You don't have to be born special to be special" when it comes to characters might be slightly unfair.

    Look at it this way. Aurics are not only above-baseline individuals already, but they also have very obvious character arcs. Fighting Aurics have a distinct option to improve their martial arts abilities, learning to make Auric Constructs, learn and train with other Fighting Aurics, and fight for justice and all that. Psychics are linked to mindscapes inescapably, one way or another, and are some of the easiest points to kick off such plots, as well as having an easier time bringing self-reflection character arcs to the forefront. Obscurics are a bit more vague due to their rarity, but they usually have an easier time defining their powers to their own self, rather than the other way around, since Obscura is both difficult to use without training and has very few trained individuals available for mentorship.

    And then there's Vierr, but we don't worry about him. He's terrible.

    With the looming prize of Gate-usage at the tall, tall mountain of character training and development, even if it's not reached within the life span of a nine-tailed fox, it's still an incredibly easy goal to point to and say, "My character should try and do that."

    Now, I'm not saying that being mundane is a downside, but it does require a bit more skill and forethought to define a long-term goal for characterization without rambling around unsure of what to do. Like the difference between Fallout 3 (which is fully open and you can wander aimlessly for days before finally getting back to the main plot) and Fallout: New Vegas (where you start very quickly, with a set goal, follow said goal, and once you're pretty much well established and you've made it to the main city, you can basically pursue any quest you want).
